11//! Pseudo-random number generators.
12//!
13//! Pseudo-random number generators are algorithms to produce apparently random
14//! numbers deterministically, and usually fairly quickly. See the documentation
15//! of the [`rngs` module] for some introduction to PRNGs.
16//!
17//! As mentioned there, PRNGs fall in two broad categories:
18//!
19//! - [basic PRNGs], primarily designed for simulations
20//! - [CSPRNGs], primarily designed for cryptography
21//!
22//! In simple terms, the basic PRNGs are often predictable; CSPRNGs should not
23//! be predictable *when used correctly*.
24//! 
25//! Contents of this documentation:
26//!
27//! 1. [The generators](#the-generators)
28//! 1. [Performance and size](#performance)
29//! 1. [Quality and cycle length](#quality)
30//! 1. [Security](#security)
31//! 1. [Extra features](#extra-features)
32//! 1. [Further reading](#further-reading)
33//!
34//!
35//! # The generators
36//!
37//! ## Basic pseudo-random number generators (PRNGs)
38//!
39//! The goal of regular, non-cryptographic PRNGs is usually to find a good
40//! balance between simplicity, quality, memory usage and performance. These
41//! algorithms are very important to Monte Carlo simulations, and also suitable
42//! for several other problems such as randomized algorithms and games (except
43//! where there is a risk of players predicting the next output value from
44//! previous values, in which case a CSPRNG should be used).
45//!
46//! Currently Rand provides only one PRNG, and not a very good one at that:
47//!
48//! | name | full name | performance | memory | quality | period | features |
49//! |------|-----------|-------------|--------|---------|--------|----------|
50//! | [`XorShiftRng`] | Xorshift 32/128 | ★★★☆☆ | 16 bytes | ★☆☆☆☆ | `u32` * 2<sup>128</sup> - 1 | — |
51//!
52// Quality stars [not rendered in documentation]:
53// 5. reserved for crypto-level (e.g. ChaCha8, ISAAC)
54// 4. good performance on TestU01 and PractRand, good theory
55//    (e.g. PCG, truncated Xorshift*)
56// 3. good performance on TestU01 and PractRand, but "falling through the
57//    cracks" or insufficient theory (e.g. SFC, Xoshiro)
58// 2. imperfect performance on tests or other limiting properties, but not
59//    terrible (e.g. Xoroshiro128+)
60// 1. clear deficiencies in test results, cycle length, theory, or other
61//    properties (e.g. Xorshift)
62//
63// Performance stars [not rendered in documentation]:
64// Meant to give an indication of relative performance. Roughly follows a log
65// scale, based on the performance of `next_u64` on a current i5/i7:
66// - 5. 8000 MB/s+
67// - 4. 4000 MB/s+
68// - 3. 2000 MB/s+
69// - 2. 1000 MB/s+
70// - 1. < 1000 MB/s
71//
72//! ## Cryptographically secure pseudo-random number generators (CSPRNGs)
73//!
74//! CSPRNGs have much higher requirements than basic PRNGs. The primary
75//! consideration is security. Performance and simplicity are also important,
76//! but in general CSPRNGs are more complex and slower than regular PRNGs.
77//! Quality is no longer a concern, as it is a requirement for a
78//! CSPRNG that the output is basically indistinguishable from true randomness
79//! since any bias or correlation makes the output more predictable.
80//!
81//! There is a close relationship between CSPRNGs and cryptographic ciphers.
82//! Any block cipher can be turned into a CSPRNG by encrypting a counter. Stream
83//! ciphers are basically a CSPRNG and a combining operation, usually XOR. This
84//! means that we can easily use any stream cipher as a CSPRNG.
85//!
86//! Rand currently provides two trustworthy CSPRNGs and two CSPRNG-like PRNGs:
87//!
88//! | name | full name |  performance | initialization | memory | predictability | forward secrecy |
89//! |------|-----------|--------------|--------------|----------|----------------|-------------------------|
90//! | [`ChaChaRng`] | ChaCha20 | ★☆☆☆☆ | fast | 136 bytes | secure | no |
91//! | [`Hc128Rng`] | HC-128 | ★★☆☆☆ | slow | 4176 bytes | secure | no |
92//! | [`IsaacRng`] | ISAAC | ★★☆☆☆ | slow | 2072 bytes | unknown | unknown |
93//! | [`Isaac64Rng`] | ISAAC-64 | ★★☆☆☆ | slow | 4136 bytes| unknown | unknown |
94//!
95//! It should be noted that the ISAAC generators are only included for
96//! historical reasons, they have been with the Rust language since the very
97//! beginning. They have good quality output and no attacks are known, but have
98//! received little attention from cryptography experts.
99//!
100//!
101//! # Performance
102//!
103//! First it has to be said most PRNGs are very fast, and will rarely be a
104//! performance bottleneck.
105//!
106//! Performance of basic PRNGs is a bit of a subtle thing. It depends a lot on
107//! the CPU architecture (32 vs. 64 bits), inlining, and also on the number of
108//! available registers. This often causes the performance to be affected by
109//! surrounding code due to inlining and other usage of registers.
110//!
111//! When choosing a PRNG for performance it is important to benchmark your own
112//! application due to interactions between PRNGs and surrounding code and
113//! dependence on the CPU architecture as well as the impact of the size of
114//! data requested. Because of all this, we do not include performance numbers
115//! here but merely a qualitative rating.
116//!
117//! CSPRNGs are a little different in that they typically generate a block of
118//! output in a cache, and pull outputs from the cache. This allows them to have
119//! good amortised performance, and reduces or completely removes the influence
120//! of surrounding code on the CSPRNG performance.
121//!
122//! ### Worst-case performance
123//! Because CSPRNGs usually produce a block of values into a cache, they have
124//! poor worst case performance (in contrast to basic PRNGs, where the
125//! performance is usually quite regular).
126//!
127//! ## State size
128//!
129//! Simple PRNGs often use very little memory, commonly only a few words, where
130//! a *word* is usually either `u32` or `u64`. This is not true for all
131//! non-cryptographic PRNGs however, for example the historically popular
132//! Mersenne Twister MT19937 algorithm requires 2.5 kB of state.
133//!
134//! CSPRNGs typically require more memory; since the seed size is recommended
135//! to be at least 192 bits and some more may be required for the algorithm,
136//! 256 bits would be approximately the minimum secure size. In practice,
137//! CSPRNGs tend to use quite a bit more, [`ChaChaRng`] is relatively small with
138//! 136 bytes of state.
139//! 
140//! ## Initialization time
141//!
142//! The time required to initialize new generators varies significantly. Many
143//! simple PRNGs and even some cryptographic ones (including [`ChaChaRng`])
144//! only need to copy the seed value and some constants into their state, and
145//! thus can be constructed very quickly. In contrast, CSPRNGs with large state
146//! require an expensive key-expansion.
147//!
148//! # Quality
149//!
150//! Many basic PRNGs are not much more than a couple of bitwise and arithmetic
151//! operations. Their simplicity gives good performance, but also means there
152//! are small regularities hidden in the generated random number stream.
153//!
154//! How much do those hidden regularities matter? That is hard to say, and
155//! depends on how the RNG gets used. If there happen to be correlations between
156//! the random numbers and the algorithm they are used in, the results can be
157//! wrong or misleading.
158//!
159//! A random number generator can be considered good if it gives the correct
160//! results in as many applications as possible. The quality of PRNG
161//! algorithms can be evaluated to some extend analytically, to determine the
162//! cycle length and to rule out some correlations. Then there are empirical
163//! test suites designed to test how well a PRNG performs on a wide range of
164//! possible uses, the latest and most complete of which are [TestU01] and
165//! [PractRand].
166//!
167//! CSPRNGs tend to be more complex, and have an explicit requirement to be
168//! unpredictable. This implies there must be no obvious correlations between
169//! output values.
170//!
171//! ### Quality stars:
172//! PRNGs with 3 stars or more should be good enough for any purpose.
173//! 1 or 2 stars may be good enough for typical apps and games, but do not work
174//! well with all algorithms.
175//!
176//! ## Period
177//!
178//! The *period* or *cycle length* of a PRNG is the number of values that can be
179//! generated after which it starts repeating the same random number stream.
180//! Many PRNGs have a fixed-size period, but for some only an expected average
181//! cycle length can be given, where the exact length depends on the seed.
182//!
183//! On today's hardware, even a fast RNG with a cycle length of *only*
184//! 2<sup>64</sup> can be used for centuries before cycling. Yet we recommend a
185//! period of 2<sup>128</sup> or more, which most modern PRNGs satisfy.
186//! Alternatively a PRNG with shorter period but support for multiple streams
187//! may be chosen. There are two reasons for this, as follows.
188//!
189//! If we see the entire period of an RNG as one long random number stream,
190//! every independently seeded RNG returns a slice of that stream. When multiple
191//! RNG are seeded randomly, there is an increasingly large chance to end up
192//! with a partially overlapping slice of the stream.
193//!
194//! If the period of the RNG is 2<sup>128</sup>, and an application consumes
195//! 2<sup>48</sup> values, it then takes about 2<sup>32</sup> random
196//! initializations to have a chance of 1 in a million to repeat part of an
197//! already used stream. This seems good enough for common usage of
198//! non-cryptographic generators, hence the recommendation of at least
199//! 2<sup>128</sup>. As an estimate, the chance of any overlap in a period of
200//! size `p` with `n` independent seeds and `u` values used per seed is
201//! approximately `1 - e^(-u * n^2 / (2 * p))`.
202//!
203//! Further, it is not recommended to use the full period of an RNG. Many
204//! PRNGs have a property called *k-dimensional equidistribution*, meaning that
205//! for values of some size (potentially larger than the output size), all
206//! possible values are produced the same number of times over the generator's
207//! period. This is not a property of true randomness. This is known as the
208//! generalized birthday problem, see the [PCG paper] for a good explanation.
209//! This results in a noticable bias on output after generating more values
210//! than the square root of the period (after 2<sup>64</sup> values for a
211//! period of 2<sup>128</sup>).
212//!
213//!
214//! # Security
215//!
216//! ## Predictability
217//!
218//! From the context of any PRNG, one can ask the question *given some previous
219//! output from the PRNG, is it possible to predict the next output value?*
220//! This is an important property in any situation where there might be an
221//! adversary.
222//!
223//! Regular PRNGs tend to be predictable, although with varying difficulty. In
224//! some cases prediction is trivial, for example plain Xorshift outputs part of
225//! its state without mutation, and prediction is as simple as seeding a new
226//! Xorshift generator from four `u32` outputs. Other generators, like
227//! [PCG](http://www.pcg-random.org/predictability.html) and truncated Xorshift*
228//! are harder to predict, but not outside the realm of common mathematics and a
229//! desktop PC.
230//!
231//! The basic security that CSPRNGs must provide is the infeasibility to predict
232//! output. This requirement is formalized as the [next-bit test]; this is
233//! roughly stated as: given the first *k* bits of a random sequence, the
234//! sequence satisfies the next-bit test if there is no algorithm able to
235//! predict the next bit using reasonable computing power.
236//!
237//! A further security that *some* CSPRNGs provide is forward secrecy:
238//! in the event that the CSPRNGs state is revealed at some point, it must be
239//! infeasible to reconstruct previous states or output. Note that many CSPRNGs
240//! *do not* have forward secrecy in their usual formulations.
241//!
242//! As an outsider it is hard to get a good idea about the security of an
243//! algorithm. People in the field of cryptography spend a lot of effort
244//! analyzing existing designs, and what was once considered good may now turn
245//! out to be weaker. Generally it is best to use algorithms well-analyzed by
246//! experts, such as those recommended by NIST or ECRYPT.
247//!
248//! ## State and seeding
249//!
250//! It is worth noting that a CSPRNG's security relies absolutely on being
251//! seeded with a secure random key. Should the key be known or guessable, all
252//! output of the CSPRNG is easy to guess. This implies that the seed should
253//! come from a trusted source; usually either the OS or another CSPRNG. Our
254//! seeding helper trait, [`FromEntropy`], and the source it uses
255//! ([`EntropyRng`]), should be secure. Additionally, [`ThreadRng`] is a CSPRNG,
256//! thus it is acceptable to seed from this (although for security applications
257//! fresh/external entropy should be preferred).
258//!
259//! Further, it should be obvious that the internal state of a CSPRNG must be
260//! kept secret. With that in mind, our implementations do not provide direct
261//! access to most of their internal state, and `Debug` implementations do not
262//! print any internal state. This does not fully protect CSPRNG state; code
263//! within the same process may read this memory (and we allow cloning and
264//! serialisation of CSPRNGs for convenience). Further, a running process may be
265//! forked by the operating system, which may leave both processes with a copy
266//! of the same generator.
267//!
268//! ## Not a crypto library
269//!
270//! It should be emphasised that this is not a cryptography library; although
271//! Rand does take some measures to provide secure random numbers, it does not
272//! necessarily take all recommended measures. Further, cryptographic processes
273//! such as encryption and authentication are complex and must be implemented
274//! very carefully to avoid flaws and resist known attacks. It is therefore
275//! recommended to use specialized libraries where possible, for example
276//! [openssl], [ring] and the [RustCrypto libraries].
277//!
278//!
279//! # Extra features
280//!
281//! Some PRNGs may provide extra features, like:
282//!
283//! - Support for multiple streams, which can help with parallel tasks.
284//! - The ability to jump or seek around in the random number stream;
285//!   with large periood this can be used as an alternative to streams.
